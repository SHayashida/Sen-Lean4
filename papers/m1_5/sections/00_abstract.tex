\begin{abstract}
A finite impossibility witness can certify that a boundary between axiom combinations is real, but does it also determine how the resulting repairs should be presented?
We show that it does not, even under a strong comparison notion.
Two lever-structured encodings of the same impossibility boundary in the Sen24 base case \((n,m)=(2,4)\) are shown to be \emph{clause-multiset equivalent} ($\equiv_{\mathsf{CM}}$): they produce identical CNF clause multisets up to variable renaming.
Both are UNSAT.
Yet their raw lever-level minimal repair families differ.
Under the natural lever correspondence that transports bundled repairs to the split lever family, the bundled singleton repair becomes a two-element set that is not inclusion-minimal on the split side, where finer person-specific singleton repairs are available instead.
The transported and actual repair families therefore do not coincide.
This is an existence theorem: clause-multiset equivalence does not guarantee canonical raw repair explanation.
The normative implication is that finite witness legitimization alone---even backed by $\equiv_{\mathsf{CM}}$---does not settle repair presentation.
Audit-oriented repair reporting requires an explicit abstraction contract specifying how repairs are to be compared or grouped across encodings.
\end{abstract}
