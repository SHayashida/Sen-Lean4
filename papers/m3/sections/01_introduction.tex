\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
M1 established an auditable finite-atlas workflow that separates Proof, Audit,
Witness, and Assumption obligations.  M1.5 then showed that this evidence
discipline does not by itself determine a canonical repair explanation:
clause-multiset-equivalent bundled and split realizations can expose different
inclusion-minimal raw lever deletions.  M3 asks the positive follow-up question:
under what declared interface and soundness conditions can repair reports
nevertheless be canonical at a contract level?

\paragraph{Thesis.}
A valid impossibility certificate does not by itself determine how repairs may
be reported. Repair reportability is a contract-relative property. Under
explicit interface and soundness conditions, grouped repair reports can be
canonical even when raw implementation repairs are not.

\paragraph{Contribution boundary.}
The contribution is not the observation that encoding granularity can change
MUS, MCS, or repair enumeration; related effects belong to the established
diagnosis and explanation literature.  The contribution is a contract-relative
theory of reportability:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item M3-A, an atomic syntactic sufficient condition;
  \item M3-B, a non-atomic sufficient condition based on group soundness;
  \item M3-C, an exactness result under deletion-monotone reference contracts;
  \item a Lean-checked abstract finite-set theorem core; and
  \item an artifact-backed Candidate B instantiation of M3-B.
\end{enumerate}

M3-B recovers report-level canonicity on Candidate B only after the
reportability contract is made explicit and \(\GroupSoundness\) is verified.
It does not erase M1.5, make raw repairs canonical, or make M3-A applicable to
the non-atomic Candidate B realization.
