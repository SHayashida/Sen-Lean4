\title{Auditable Claim Boundaries for Finite Social-Choice Artifacts:\\
A Sen24 Case Study}
\author{Shunya Hayashida}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Formal artifacts can certify that a decision rule is impossible, inconsistent,
or repairable. But they do not automatically determine which repair or
reporting claim is licensed by the evidence. This preprint studies that
problem in Sen24, a small two-voter, four-alternative social-choice setting
where the relevant finite artifact can be audited completely. The case is
small enough to inspect exhaustively while still containing nontrivial repair
and reporting choices. Under the declared M4 encoding, it records a finite
audit of the repair and reporting structure, including the 48-cell
paper-facing certificate and grouped-report checks. It also identifies the
central right-condition target and connects it to a Lean-backed semantic
definition, with a sufficient bridge from two fixed conditions to
\code{MINLIB}. The paper deliberately separates those supported statements
from future Level C obligations, including Python/CNF artifact correctness and
semantic-to-CNF correctness. Its contribution is a claim-boundary methodology
for finite social-choice artifacts, not an
end-to-end verification result or a new general theorem of Sen.
\end{abstract}
