\section{Introduction}

When a computer reports that no decision rule can meet every requirement we
gave it, the practical question is larger than whether the run was performed
correctly. We need to know which requirement is responsible, which change would
repair the conflict, which parts of that answer are backed by the recorded
evidence, and which parts depend on modeling choices outside the check. This
paper studies that problem in a small but complete social-choice example: it
enumerates the possible repairs, checks the recorded evidence, connects one
central condition to a small formal proof, and separates those supported
results from stronger correctness claims. It does not claim to solve social
choice in general or to prove that every layer of the computer encoding is
correct.

Repair and reporting claims can exceed what an artifact supports when the
evidence layer, the modeling layer, and the prose interpretation are collapsed
into one statement. A finite certificate may justify an internal report under a
declared encoding without also proving that every generated clause, every
profile representation, or every assignment semantics has been connected to a
separate formal theory.

The running case is Sen24: a two-voter, four-alternative social-choice setting
used here as a small complete artifact. The purpose of the case is not to
re-prove the general theorem of Sen. The purpose is to make the reportable
claim boundary visible in a setting where the finite evidence can be audited
completely.

The paper's contribution has two layers. At the methodology level, it argues
that formal impossibility artifacts should be audited for the repair and
reporting claims they are entitled to make, not only for local correctness of a
run. At the technical M4 level, under the declared M4 encoding, it records the
finite audit, identifies a Lean-backed semantic target for the central
condition, and leaves artifact-level semantic-to-CNF correctness as a future
correctness obligation.

This paper does not claim Python encoder correctness, generated CNF artifact
correctness, full semantic-to-CNF correctness, a new general Sen theorem, full
selector-free encoding equivalence to \code{MINLIB}, or a structural proof of
the Mask-Shape Collapse Law. It is not an end-to-end verification result.

The scaffolded contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item a plain-language claim-boundary framing for finite social-choice
  artifacts;
\item a finite audit statement under the declared M4 encoding;
\item a Lean-backed semantic target for the central right condition;
\item an explicit separation between certified finite claims and future
  correctness obligations;
\item a reusable outline for safe abstract and introduction wording that avoids
  overclaiming beyond the evidence.
\end{itemize}
